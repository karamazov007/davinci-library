The starting point

Why I'm doing this

Meditation is not a way to feel calmer for twenty minutes a day. It's a vehicle toward seeing the nature of mind, feeling, and change directly — and being changed by that seeing. This page is the "why" I keep coming back to when the practice gets dull.

01 · The frame

The vehicle, not the view from it

You ride a bike to get from A to B. Feeling the wind on your face is real, and pleasant — but it's a side effect, not the reason you rode. Meditation is the same. Stress relief, better sleep, a quieter mind: all real, all side effects. The actual destination is insight — seeing, not merely believing, that experience is impermanent, that clinging to the changing is what hurts, and that there is no fixed self at the centre to defend.

"If you meditate to feel calm, you've parked the bike and sat on it enjoying the breeze — without going anywhere."

Calm (samatha) still matters — but as the steadiness that lets insight (vipassanā) arise, not as the prize itself. The prize is paññā: wisdom that reorganises you at the root.

02 · Honesty

Why I'm actually here

I'm restless. Anxious more often than I'd like. I don't feel that who I am is enough. Emotional weather knocks me around. I'm rarely present — I'm in the future I'm planning or the past I'm re-editing, almost never in the room I'm standing in. I want to detach from the churn without going cold — to stay fully engaged with my life while holding it in an open hand instead of a clenched fist.

The gap I'm closing: I already understand these ideas intellectually. That changes nothing — anxiety doesn't live in my conclusions, it lives below them. Which is exactly why this has to be practised, not just read. Understanding is the map; the territory only yields to walking it.

03 · The decision

Stop building breadth. Build depth.

I've sampled the buffet — Stoicism, Nietzsche, absurdism, Advaita, Zen, nihilism. Comparing the rims of many wells forever is its own kind of avoidance; you only find water by climbing into one and digging. The restlessness that pushes me to the next philosophy is itself the taṇhā the path is meant to cure — craving wearing the costume of curiosity.

I don't have to prove this tradition is "true" before committing. Depth is the only instrument that can test it — and the Buddha said as much: don't accept it on authority, test it in your own experience (Kālāma Sutta, AN 3.65). I commit so that I can run the test.

04 · How I'll work

Just enough theory to practise — then practise

My old failure mode: dive into theory because learning flatters me, and skip the doing that actually delivers consequences. So I'm inverting it. Surface theory → practise → hit a wall → bring back the specific doubt → learn only the theory that dissolves that wall → back to practice. The Buddha endorsed exactly this: the man shot with a poisoned arrow who refuses treatment until every question is answered simply dies (Cūḷamālunkya Sutta, MN 63). The teaching is a raft for crossing, not a load to carry (Alagaddūpama Sutta, MN 22).

The loop, in one line: read a little → sit → notice what breaks → ask → refine → sit again. The theory earns its place only by serving tomorrow's practice.
05 · The build

Two practices, one path

1 · Training meditation

The morning sit. Breath as anchor (ānāpānasati). Feel the breath; the mind wanders; the instant I notice is the rep; return. This builds the muscle — the path factor of Right Concentration.

2 · Being present

The all-day practice. Anchor to the senses in ordinary acts, single-task on purpose, use transitions as bells, catch the feeling-tone in the wild, and note the mind's time-travel. This is Right Mindfulness off the cushion.

The sit is the gym; life is the sport. The formal minutes aren't the mindfulness — they're the training for it. Goal isn't unbroken presence (chasing that is its own anxious project) — it's shrinking the interval between forgetting and remembering.

06 · The ramp

Progressive overload

Consistency beats duration. Daily and short outperforms occasional and long. Hold each rung until it feels normal, then step up.

Cattāri Ariyasaccāni · the four noble truths

The Four Noble Truths

The Buddha's first teaching after awakening, given at the Deer Park at Isipatana (Sarnath) to five ascetics. Not a creed — a diagnosis. Each truth carries a task: something to do, not merely believe.

The structure

Read it as a doctor reads a case

Symptom
Dukkha
There is a pervasive unsatisfactoriness.
Cause
Samudaya
It arises from craving.
Prognosis
Nirodha
It can cease.
Treatment
Magga
The Eightfold Path is the cure.

The whole architecture hangs off this diagnostic logic. Miss it and the truths read as gloomy claims; see it and they become a set of instructions.

The four

Each truth and its task

1

There is dukkha

dukkha — unsatisfactoriness, unease
Task · to be understood

"Suffering" is too strong; dukkha is closer to a wheel off its axle — a ride that judders. It runs from acute pain to the faint hum of "not quite enough" even in good moments. The task is to look clearly, the way a doctor looks at a symptom — not deny it, not flee it.

2

Dukkha has an origin

samudaya — the arising, i.e. taṇhā (craving)
Task · to be abandoned

The friction isn't caused by the world; it's caused by the thirst — the demand that this moment be other than it is. Beneath it sits avijjā (ignorance): the mistaken belief that grasping impermanent things can give lasting satisfaction.

3

Dukkha can cease

nirodha — cessation; nibbāna, the "blowing out"
Task · to be realised

Since the cause is craving and not the world, there's a real exit — available from the inside. Nibbāna is the extinguishing of the fires of greed, hatred and delusion. Not annihilation, not a place: the peace that remains when compulsive grasping stops. This can't be believed into being; it must be directly experienced.

4

There is a path to that cessation

magga — the Noble Eightfold Path, the Middle Way
Task · to be developed

The Middle Way, struck between sensual indulgence and harsh self-mortification — both dead ends the Buddha had personally tested. You don't believe the path; you cultivate it, repeatedly, until it matures. → see the next tab.

Zoom in · truth 1

Three kinds of dukkha

Ordinary
dukkha-dukkha

Plain pain — sickness, ageing, injury, loss, death. The obvious layer.

Of change
vipariṇāma-dukkha

Even pleasures hurt, because they end. The pleasant carries the seed of its own loss.

Of conditioning
saṅkhāra-dukkha

The subtlest: nothing put-together and impermanent can deliver lasting satisfaction. The background hum — my restlessness at its root.

Zoom in · truth 2

Three kinds of craving

For pleasure
kāma-taṇhā

Wanting more of the pleasant — the notification I hope contains something good.

For becoming
bhava-taṇhā

Wanting to be, to continue, to become someone — the engine of my ambition and my "I need the result now."

For non-becoming
vibhava-taṇhā

Wanting to get rid of, to make something stop — aversion pushed to the extreme; the wish to annihilate a feeling rather than meet it.

All three are the same move in different clothes: refusing the present.
The completeness claim

Three phases × four truths = twelve aspects

In the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta, the Buddha said his knowledge of each truth was complete only when it had turned through three phases. Full awakening is knowing all twelve.

Ariya Aṭṭhaṅgika Magga · the noble eightfold path

The Eightfold Path

Eight factors, not eight steps — developed together, each supporting the others. Bundled into three trainings. The prefix sammā means "right," better read as "complete" or "skilful."

The map

One wheel, three trainings

Wisdom · paññā
  1. Right View — seeing the four truths
  2. Right Intention — renunciation, goodwill, harmlessness
Ethical conduct · sīla
  1. Right Speech
  2. Right Action
  3. Right Livelihood
Mental discipline · samādhi
  1. Right Effort
  2. Right Mindfulness
  3. Right Concentration
My two practices live in the samādhi group: the morning sit is Right Concentration (8); all-day presence is Right Mindfulness (7). That's the engine room of the whole path.

Wisdom — the orientation

paññā
1

Right View

sammā diṭṭhi

Seeing things as they are: the four truths, cause and effect (karma), and the three marks (impermanence, unsatisfactoriness, not-self). Comes in two grades — a working, provisional view (enough to start walking) and a direct insight that results from the path. It's the forerunner that aims the other seven.

2

Right Intention

sammā saṅkappa

Where the heart is aimed — three strands: renunciation (inclining to let go, not grasp), goodwill (mettā, non-ill-will), and harmlessness (compassion, non-cruelty). Right view sees; right intention leans.

Ethical conduct — the foundation

sīla

Not commandments from above, but the conditions that keep the mind quiet enough to concentrate. A mind rattled by its own harm can't settle at dawn.

3

Right Speech

sammā vācā

Abstaining from lying, divisive speech, harsh speech, and idle chatter. The positive bar: speak only what is true, beneficial, and kind/timely.

4

Right Action

sammā kammanta

Abstaining from killing/harming, from taking what isn't given, and from sexual misconduct. Positively: non-harm, honesty, respect.

5

Right Livelihood

sammā ājīva

Earning a living without causing harm. The principle: your work shouldn't require you to damage others to eat.

Mental discipline — the engine

samādhi
6

Right Effort

sammā vāyāma

Four moves: prevent unwholesome states not yet arisen, abandon those present, arouse wholesome states not yet arisen, maintain those present. The key is balance — the lute-string simile: too tight and it snaps, too slack and it won't sound (Soṇa Sutta, AN 6.55).

7

Right Mindfulness

sammā sati

The four foundations of mindfulness: body, feeling-tones, states of mind, and mental phenomena — held in clear, present awareness (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta, MN 10). This is my all-day presence practice.

8

Right Concentration

sammā samādhi

The gathered, unified mind — classically the four jhānas, progressively deeper states of collected attention, developed through mindfulness of breathing (Ānāpānasati Sutta, MN 118). This is my morning training sit.

Concentration and mindfulness are two hands washing each other: concentration steadies the beam; mindfulness knows what the beam lights up.

Tipiṭaka · the Pāli Canon

Sources & the Canon

Everything above traces to the Tipiṭaka ("three baskets") — the earliest complete body of Buddhist scripture, preserved in Pāli. This is the map of where to find things, and the running list of every text cited in this hub.

Structure

The three baskets

🧭 Vinaya Piṭaka
The basket of discipline
  • Rules and origin-stories of the monastic community (Sangha)
  • Least relevant to our practice — but the ethical spine (sīla) grows from the same soil
📜 Sutta Piṭaka
The basket of discourses
  • The Buddha's talks — where almost everything we use lives
  • Divided into five Nikāyas (collections) →
🧠 Abhidhamma Piṭaka
The basket of higher doctrine
  • Systematic analysis of mind and matter
  • Advanced; a later map of the same territory the suttas describe experientially
Inside the Sutta Piṭaka

The five Nikāyas — and the citation codes

Every reference in this hub uses a standard code: the Nikāya abbreviation + the discourse number. Learn these four and you can find anything.

DN · Dīgha Nikāya

The long discourses (34 suttas). e.g. the long Satipaṭṭhāna, DN 22.

MN · Majjhima Nikāya

The middle-length discourses (152). Home of MN 10, MN 22, MN 63, MN 118.

SN · Saṃyutta Nikāya

The connected discourses, grouped by theme. Home of SN 45.8 and SN 56.11.

AN · Aṅguttara Nikāya

The numbered discourses, grouped by count. Home of AN 3.65, AN 6.55.

The fifth is the Khuddaka Nikāya ("minor collection") — which despite the name holds famous texts like the Dhammapada.

Orientation

How to actually read the Canon

Don't start at page one — the Canon isn't built to be read cover to cover. Start with the discourse behind whatever question is live on the cushion. For us that's a short spine: SN 56.11 (the whole diagnosis), SN 45.8 (the path), then MN 118 and MN 10 when the sitting itself raises questions. Everything else is looked up as doubts arise — the same practice-first loop from the "Why This" tab.

Translation note: comparing two translations of the same line (say Bhikkhu Sujato and Ṭhānissaro) often teaches more than a commentary — the gap between them is where the interpretive choices live.
