Layer 1 · First Principles

How Your Brain Actually Works

Two systems. They do not cooperate under stress. Everything downstream of this depends on understanding it.
system 1 · ancient · milliseconds

The Limbic Brain

Evolved to keep you alive. Operates faster than conscious thought. Cannot distinguish between a lion and a skilled bowling friend — it detects threat to survival or status, period.

When it fires, it does three things simultaneously and you cannot override them consciously:

releases adrenaline + cortisol
redirects blood to large muscles (legs, core)
suppresses prefrontal cortex activity
system 2 · slow · deliberate · fragile

The Prefrontal Cortex

Your strategy brain. Holds technique, focus, self-talk, your carefully built approach. Works beautifully at moderate arousal — actively enhances performance there.

Past a fear threshold, cortisol and norepinephrine literally reduce PFC synaptic connectivity. This is not metaphor.

your mantras live here ← taken offline under threat
your technique lives here ← cut off under threat
your focus instructions live here ← inaccessible

This is why your mantras fail in the moment. The mantra is a PFC tool. The PFC has been taken offline. You cannot think your way out of a limbic hijack using PFC tools.

Layer 1 · First Principles

The Arousal-Performance Curve

The most important structural fact in your entire problem. Performance is not linear with arousal.
Yerkes-Dodson Law — the inverted U
low arousal

Underperformance

Bored, not caring, no edge. Execution is flat.

moderate · the goal

Peak performance

Engaged, alert, slight pressure. PFC fully online. This is the target.

high arousal

Catastrophic collapse

Fear, panic, pounding heart. PFC offline. Technique inaccessible.

The goal is never zero arousal. Nervousness is not the enemy. Excess nervousness past the tipping point is.
Layer 1 · First Principles

The Self-Amplifying Feedback Loop

Nine sequential steps. Each feeds the next. Once started, you cannot think your way out from inside it.
01

Threat trigger

Friend bowls well. Bad streak begins. Go-kart speed feels uncontrollable. Social evaluator appears.

02

Amygdala fires — ~200ms

Before you consciously register fear. Faster than thought. Already happening.

03

Adrenaline release — 5–10 seconds

Hypothalamus signals adrenal glands. The chemistry is now in your bloodstream.

04

Heart rate spikes, breathing shallows

Blood moves away from fine motor muscles — your bowling arm, your throttle foot.

05

Cortisol releases — 30–60 seconds

Sustains the stress state. PFC synaptic connectivity actively reduced.

06

Technique, strategy, focus: inaccessible

Everything you depend on lives in the PFC. The PFC has been suppressed.

07

Dark mind, paralysis, inability to concentrate

You want to focus. You cannot. This is not weakness — it's suppressed neurochemistry.

08

Poor execution

Bad ball. Bad corner. Bad rally. The thing you feared happening now happens.

09

New threat input → loop reinforces itself

Poor execution becomes new evidence. New evidence refires the amygdala. Back to step 2.

This is a self-amplifying feedback loop. Once it starts, it feeds itself. The intervention points are before step 1, between steps 8 and 9 — not during the spiral itself.

Layer 3 · Optimization Targets

What You're Actually Optimizing For

Three categories. Each derived from the first principles above — not guesses.
PHYSIOLOGICALBody signals to manage
Heart rate
Stay below ~140–150 bpm during play
Above this threshold, fine motor control degrades sharply
Breathing pattern
Exhale longer than inhale, always
Short fast inhales = more adrenaline. Long exhales = parasympathetic brake
Muscle tension
Shoulders, bowling arm, throttle foot — loose, not gripped
Tension restricts movement which causes technique breakdown
COGNITIVEMind signals to manage
Attentional focus
External, singular, immediate
Internal focus and wide focus both destroy execution — proven in sports science
Thought horizon
One ball. One lap. Nothing else.
Projecting outcomes floods the PFC with threat simulations before you've even started
Threat reappraisal
Reclassify "evaluation" as "experiment"
Changes what the amygdala responds to at the source — before the chemistry starts
BEHAVIOURALPatterns to manage
Starting arousal
Enter activity already in moderate zone
Cold start into fear is harder to manage — if you start lower, the spike lands lower
Bad streak response
Fixed reset routine within 10 seconds
Prevents the spiral completing its second loop — interrupts before it compounds
Exposure gradient
Each session slightly harder than comfort, not maximally scary
This is how the amygdala learns — gradual signal updating, not flooding and white-knuckling
Layer 4 · Strategies

Five Strategies to Meet the Targets

Each strategy addresses specific targets. Each has a principle and a practice.
S1Pre-Regulate Before the Window Closesphysiological · before the trigger
The only reliable intervention point is before adrenaline is circulating. Once it starts, you're managing damage. Before it starts, you're setting baseline.
principleIf your panic threshold is 100 and the trigger adds 60 arousal — start at 50 and you hit 110 (collapse). Start at 30 and you hit 90 (high but functional). Lower the baseline.
doPhysiological sigh — double inhale through nose, long slow exhale through mouth. 4–5 cycles. 10–15 minutes before the session. Stanford research: single fastest method to reduce physiological arousal. Done privately — bathroom, car, corner.
notScrolling phone, joking loudly, watching others perform — all of these raise baseline arousal before you've even started.
S2Reappraise the Threat at Sourcecognitive · before the trigger
The amygdala responds to the meaning you assign to a situation, not the situation itself. You cannot argue with it directly. But you can change what you feed into it.
principleTwo people in the same go-kart — one terrified, one thrilled. Same physical experience. Different meaning assigned. Different amygdala response. The meaning is the variable.
doBefore the session, explicitly reframe: not "I need to perform well" but "I am here to collect data on what happens when I push slightly harder." Not "my friend will see how I bowl" but "my friend being here gives me realistic pressure training."
notThis is not positive thinking — "it'll be fine." It's reclassification. Giving the amygdala a different input. Experiment vs test of worth.
S3Install a Fixed Reset Routinebehavioural · during · loop interruption
You need to interrupt the loop between bad event and interpretation with a fixed physical action — before the streak becomes a story, before the story becomes more adrenaline.
principleThe content of the routine matters less than the fact that it is identical every time. Your brain associates the routine with "reset" and the next delivery gets a clean start.
doBowling: walk back to ball return → slow breath → two-hand touch on ball → eyes to target arrow only → go. Same every time. Always.
doKarting: after bad corner → deliberate exhale → eyes to exit of next corner (not the wall) → loosen grip slightly → press. Same every time. Always.
S4Graduated Exposure With Genuine Mastery Checkpointsbehavioural · between sessions
Enduring fear ≠ updating fear. You've done go-karting many times and still feel the same pounding because you jump to maximum scary and white-knuckle it. Your brain files this as "survived danger" — not "that wasn't dangerous."
notExposure works only when you comfortably master one level before proceeding. The amygdala updates its threat classification only when each level becomes genuinely boring.

S5Build an Evidence Bank, Not Affirmationscognitive · after sessions · identity
You cannot tell yourself "I am someone who competes and grows" when your actual recent evidence is bad streaks and fear paralysis. Your brain will reject it. Durable confidence is built from a specific memory bank of real experiences.
doAfter every session — good or bad — write one sentence: "Today I did [specific action] even though [specific fear I felt]."
doExamples: "I held 70% throttle through the long straight even though my heart was pounding." / "I picked up the ball after three bad frames and executed my pre-shot routine even though I wanted to stop."
notNot "I did well" or "I performed badly." Only: what you did in spite of the fear. These are not performance successes. They are evidence that you moved despite fear.
