01Why concept visualization?

Reading gives you information; it does not give you a model. The gap between "I read that book" and "I understand it well enough to use and teach it" is bridged by structure — and structure is exactly what a diagram makes visible.

05How to mix & match them

The one rule that matters: pick the diagram by the relationship, not the topic. Ask "what is the dominant relationship here?" — hierarchy (part/whole), sequence (time/cause), network (mutual influence), comparison (overlap/difference), or dimension (position on axes). That answer picks your base diagram. Real topics contain several relationships at once, so you layer and nest types.

Nest: one type as the container, another inside each node. A mind map of a whole book where each branch opens into its own flowchart or 2×2 matrix. This is the classic "master map."

Sequence a series: place small diagrams on a timeline — one triad or cycle per era — to watch a concept evolve.

Keep three altitudes of the same topic: a one-glance framework (triad/pyramid) for recall, a concept map for relationships, and a network for how it links to everything else you know. Revise top-down.

Overlay encodings: add a second meaning to any diagram with colour (theme/source), size (importance) and edge style (strong vs. weak, causes vs. correlates). A plain network becomes a systems map the instant you add +/− to the edges.

A reliable personal workflow: (1) dump every highlight and margin note as raw nodes; (2) cluster them into 4–7 groups — that's your first hierarchy; (3) name the relationship inside each cluster and pick its diagram; (4) draw the links between clusters — those cross-links are where real understanding lives; (5) compress to a single framework you can redraw from memory. If you can redraw it blank, you own it.
