The Weak Partner
Protocol

Your partner's game is not your department. The moment you start monitoring his errors, you've split your attention — and split attention in a fast game is fatal to your own performance.

// the default trap

Monitor the partner

Reacting to every error. Attention split between your game and his. You play worse and get frustrated.

→
// the reframe

Accept the constraint

His game is outside your control. Your only job is maximising what happens on your side of the equation.

Accept the Real Constraint First

You can't change it, so stop being surprised by it.
// honest reality check
You are effectively playing 1v2
for significant stretches of this game.

Once you accept that — truly accept it, not just intellectually — you stop being surprised and frustrated every time it happens. You start planning around it instead. Surprise is what makes it draining. Acceptance is what makes it manageable.

This is not a morale speech. It is a tactical reframe. Your strategy changes the moment you stop hoping your partner will perform and start designing around the assumption that he won't.

Five Steps — Applied Inside a Live Game

Run these simultaneously, not sequentially.
STEP 1Accept the Real Constraintbefore you even step on court

Privately decide, before the session starts: "I am playing 1v2 today. My partner's errors are not surprises — they are the baseline." This kills the frustration loop at the root.

The frustration in these sessions almost always comes from a gap between expectation and reality. Close the gap at the expectation end, not the reality end. You can't change him. You can change what you expect.

↑
STEP 2Control the Serve Aggressivelythe one moment you have full control

In doubles, the serve is the one point of complete control in every rally. Use it deliberately — don't just serve and hope.

✗ automatic serve
Serve to whoever

Return lands wherever. Partner gets targeted. You scramble.

✓ intentional serve
Serve to the weaker opponent

Or serve so the return angle naturally comes back toward your zone, not his.

This requires one deliberate thought per serve — not complicated, just intentional. The opponents will start targeting your partner anyway; engineer the serve so returns come back to you first.

↑
STEP 3Expand Your Territory — Quietlypoach more than you normally would

In a functional doubles team, you respect positional boundaries. With a weak partner, quietly expand your coverage zone. Cover the middle aggressively. Take every shot that's remotely reachable.

STEP 4Engineer the Rally — Stay In Itshot selection when you have the ball

When you do get the shuttle or ball, prioritise shots that either end the rally cleanly or return the ball toward your zone — not your partner's. You're engineering the rally to keep yourself involved.

// target selection logic
Shot that ends the rally
→ best
Shot that pulls return toward your side
→ good
Shot that puts return toward partner's zone
→ avoid

Specifically: target the opponent who tends to return toward your zone. Watch where each opponent naturally places their returns in the first 5 minutes, then exploit that pattern to keep the shuttle coming back to you.

↑
STEP 5Let Points Go — Cleanlythe counterintuitive one

When your partner makes an error that loses a point, reset immediately. No body language. No glance. No pause. Treat it exactly like a net cord that went against you — just bad luck, nothing more.

🧠
// why this actually matters — two reasons

For you: every second you spend internally reacting is a second you are not resetting, reading the opponents, or preparing your next serve. Your emotional state is a performance resource — don't spend it on what you can't change.

For him: a weak player who starts feeling the pressure of a frustrated partner gets worse, not better. Your visible composure keeps him functional instead of frozen.

This is not about being a good person. It is the purely selfish, performance-based reason to let it go.

The Internal Scorecard Shift

Stop measuring your session against the wrong number.
✗ old scorecard

The actual score

  • Lagging indicator — tells you what happened, not why
  • Half determined by partner you can't control
  • Produces frustration with no actionable output
  • Makes losing sessions feel worthless
✓ new scorecard

Your process metrics

  • Did I handle every shot that came to me cleanly?
  • Did I serve with intention each time?
  • Did I stay calm after his errors?
  • Did I expand my territory and intercept what I could?

If you go 0–21 but executed your own game well and stayed composed throughout — that is genuinely a better session than winning scrappily while being mentally scattered.

// the reframe that converts frustration into training
Privately decide before the session:
"This is a session in playing well under pressure and distraction."

That converts a frustrating constraint into a deliberate training condition. Playing well despite a weak partner and an uphill scoreline is a harder mental test than a clean game with an equal partner. If you can hold your composure and structure through that, you are building something real.

// the honest caveat
This doesn't make him a better partner.
It makes you a harder opponent to break.

All of this is in service of one thing: staying useful for the duration of the session. You will still lose points because of him. You will still lose matches you might have won with a better partner. The protocol doesn't fix that. What it does is ensure that his weakness doesn't also degrade your game — which is the only thing actually in your control.

His game is not your department. Your game always is.

Accept the 1v2 — control the serve — expand your territory — engineer the rally — reset cleanly — then score yourself on the only metrics that belong to you.
